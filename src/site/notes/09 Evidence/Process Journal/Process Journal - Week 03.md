## Annotated Bibliographies

**Jenkins, H. (2006). _Convergence culture: Where old and new media collide_. New York University Press.**

Jenkins explores how media audiences move across platforms and participate in meaning-making rather than simply consuming content. The book is useful for understanding transmedia storytelling because it explains how stories, brands, and fan cultures operate across different media forms. Its strength is that it connects media theory with audience behaviour, participation, and cultural change. For my project, this source helps frame multimodal experience design as more than adaptation across platforms. It supports the idea that a speculative world can be distributed across media while still feeling connected through audience engagement, participation, and shared meaning.

**Wolf, M. J. P. (2012). _Building imaginary worlds: The theory and history of subcreation_. Routledge.**

Wolf examines worldbuilding as a creative and theoretical practice, arguing that imaginary worlds can be understood as systems with their own structures, histories, geographies, cultures, and rules. This source is highly relevant because my project is concerned with designing a speculative world rather than only telling a single linear story. Its usefulness is in treating worlds as coherent designed environments that can extend across media and invite exploration. For my research, Wolf provides a foundation for thinking about how audiences inhabit, interpret, and move through fictional worlds as experience spaces.

**Ryan, M.-L., & Thon, J.-N. (Eds.). (2014). _Storyworlds across media: Toward a media-conscious narratology_. University of Nebraska Press.**

Ryan and Thon bring together approaches to narrative that consider how storyworlds change when they move across different media. The text is useful because it shifts attention away from narrative as only plot or text and instead considers how media form shapes the experience of a storyworld. This is relevant to my project because I am exploring how speculative worlds can be experienced through interactive, visual, spatial, and participatory formats. The source supports my argument that each medium contributes differently to world experience, rather than simply repeating the same story in another format.

**Salen, K., & Zimmerman, E. (2003). _Rules of play: Game design fundamentals_. MIT Press.**

Salen and Zimmerman provide a foundational framework for understanding games as systems of rules, play, and meaning. Their concept of meaningful play is especially relevant because it explains how player actions become significant within a designed system. This source is useful for my project because interactive narrative depends on more than content; it depends on whether the audience’s choices, movement, and participation feel meaningful. For my research, this text helps connect game design theory to experience design by showing how systems, rules, feedback, and interaction shape the user’s sense of agency.

**Schell, J. (2019). _The art of game design: A book of lenses_ (3rd ed.). CRC Press.**

Schell presents game design as a process of looking at an experience through multiple “lenses,” including story, mechanics, aesthetics, technology, players, and emotion. This is useful because my project also requires a multi-perspective design approach rather than a single disciplinary method. The book is practical as well as theoretical, offering ways to evaluate whether an interactive experience is coherent, engaging, and meaningful. For my capstone, Schell supports the development of a practice-led framework by showing how creative decisions can be tested through different design questions and experiential priorities.

**Murray, J. H. (1997). _Hamlet on the holodeck: The future of narrative in cyberspace_. Free Press.**

Murray explores how digital environments change narrative by making stories procedural, participatory, spatial, and encyclopaedic. This source is important because it provides an early but still influential framework for understanding interactive digital storytelling. Its relevance to my project is in its focus on the audience as an active participant inside a designed narrative environment. Murray helps support my argument that digital narrative is not only about branching plot, but about creating spaces where users can explore, act, interpret, and feel present within a storyworld.
