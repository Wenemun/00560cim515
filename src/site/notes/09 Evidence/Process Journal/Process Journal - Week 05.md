Thoughts on the ethics training module:

My intuitive response would probably come down to inputs that are not actually outlined in the scenario. The trolley problem presents itself as clean and logical, but real instinct is messy. Maybe the “one person” reminds me of someone I know. Maybe their age, appearance, vulnerability, or expression changes how I respond without me consciously deciding that. So even before I apply an ethical principle, my instinct may already be shaped by emotional and subconscious factors.

I would also bring my own values into it. For example, if I valued knowledge, lived experience, or personal stories more highly, I might choose to save five elderly people and sacrifice a child, even though many people might instinctively see the child as the more “valuable” life because they have more future ahead of them. That shows how quickly the problem becomes less about numbers and more about what kinds of human value I prioritise.

There is also the question of responsibility. If I pull the switch, I have made an active choice that leads to someone’s death, and I would have to live with that. But if I refuse to act, that is still a choice. Is inaction really morally neutral, or does failing to act make me just as culpable? If I do nothing, is that because I believe it is wrong to directly cause death, or because I am afraid of being responsible? Does that make me principled, cowardly, or self-preserving?

Other people’s perceptions would also matter, even if I do not want them to. How do I want to be seen? As brave? Rational? Compassionate? Innocent? And if that affects my decision, does that make the choice vain, or just human? It makes me wonder whether ethical action is ever completely separate from identity and social pressure.

For me, hypothetical ethical dilemmas like the trolley problem is asking "who do I want to be", but that is also part of the problem. It creates space for virtue signalling. I can imagine myself as brave, rational, compassionate, or principled, but that does not mean I would act that way in the moment. Thinking about virtue does not make you virtuous. It may only reveal the version of yourself you want to present.

Character is less about who you are trying to be, and more about what you actually do in the moment. When the decision needs to be immediate, uncomfortable, and irreversible. The action matters more than the imagined identity behind it.

---

Singer’s argument is challenging because he claims there is no real ethical difference between saving a drowning child in front of me and helping a starving child on the other side of the world. I can accept that distance should not make a life worth less.

But I also know there is a difference in the reality of action. I have jumped into a river fully clothed to save a child I did not directly know, and I wrecked my phone doing it. In that moment, there was no abstract calculation. There was a child in danger, and I acted - I think anyone in the same position would do the same thing - I was just in the right place at the right time.

Distance does change the situation. It happens through systems. If I can feed a child for a dollar a month, which child gets that dollar? Who decides? Do I hand that decision to a charity? I have worked with charities and seen how much campaign money can go into marketing, wages, and administration rather than direct help. So the issue is not only whether I care, but whether I trust the system delivering that care. Morally, distance may not matter. Practically, it changes everything.

Giving money is messier. Through a charity, I HAVE to trust the system. Directly, I have to ask whether I am helping or enabling harm. I usually offer groceries instead, because it tests whether they need a feed or need a fix. In my experience, very few people accept the loaf of bread, can of soup, or pack of ramen. So the issue is not just whether my action helps, but whether my inaction hurts. Not that I don't care.
